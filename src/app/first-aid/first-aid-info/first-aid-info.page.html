Step 1: Assess the Situation

Ensure your safety and the safety of others around you. Approach the dog carefully. If the dog is unresponsive, check for breathing and a pulse.

Step 2: Check for Breathing

Place your ear close to the dog's nose and mouth, and watch for chest movement. Feel for airflow on your cheek. If the dog is not breathing or only gasping, it indicates a breathing emergency.

Step 3: Check for Pulse

Find the femoral pulse inside the dog's hind leg, close to the groin area. If you cannot detect a pulse or it's weak and irregular, the dog requires immediate CPR.

Please remember to administer CPR to a dog only if it's not breathing, unconscious, and shows no pulse; attempting CPR on a responsive or healthy dog could be harmful.


Step 4: Lay Your Dog on a Flat Surface

Lay the dog on his/her side (either is fine) on a flat surface. You will need to stand or kneel beside the dog. For barrel-chested dogs like Bulldogs, it is also appropriate to place the dog on his/her back.

Step 5: Perform Chest Compressions

- For small dogs: Use the heel of one hand to perform chest compressions at the point where their elbow touches the chest. Compress the chest about 1 to 1.5 inches (2.5 to 4 cm) deep.

- For medium to large dogs: Place both hands on the widest part of the rib cage and compress the chest about 2 to 3 inches (5 to 7.5 cm) deep.

Keep your elbows straight and use your body weight to administer compressions. Aim for a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute, which is roughly the beat of the song "Staying Alive."

Step 6: Artificial Respiration

Respiration After 30 compressions, provide artificial respiration to the dog to supply oxygen to their lungs.

- For small dogs: Gently close their mouth and cover their nose with your mouth, giving a breath that makes their chest rise.

- For medium to large dogs: Close their mouth, extend their neck to straighten the airway, and breathe into their nose until the chest rises.

Step 7: Continue Cycles

Alternate between 30 chest compressions and 2 breaths until the dog starts breathing on its own or until you reach a veterinarian for further medical attention.

Please remember that your pet's likelihood of surviving with resuscitation is very low. However, in an emergency it may give your pet its only chance.

First Aid for a Poisoned Dog


Step 1: Assess the Situation

Before approaching your dog, ensure your safety. If you suspect the poison may be harmful to you, use protective gear such as gloves to handle the dog.

Step 2: Remove the Dog from the Source

If the poison is still present and you can safely do so, move your dog away from the area to prevent further exposure.

Step 3: Check for Vital Signs

Assess your dog's vital signs - check for breathing, pulse, and responsiveness. If your dog is not breathing or unconscious, begin CPR if you are trained to do so. If there's no pulse, initiate chest compressions along with rescue breaths for dogs.

Step 4: Contact a Veterinarian

Call your regular veterinarian or the nearest emergency veterinary clinic immediately. Describe the situation and let them know that you suspect your dog has been poisoned. Follow their instructions closely.

Step 5: Do Not Induce Vomiting

DO NOT induce vomiting in your dog unless specifically instructed to do so by a veterinarian. Some substances can cause more harm if vomited back up.

Step 6: Observe Symptoms

Keep a close eye on your dog's symptoms and behavior. Look for signs such as vomiting, diarrhea, excessive drooling, tremors, seizures, weakness, difficulty breathing, or changes in behavior.

Step 7: Gather Information

If possible, gather information about the suspected poison. Check for any containers, labels, or remnants of the substance. Note any exposure time and quantity ingested if known. This information can assist the veterinarian in determining the appropriate treatment.

Step 8: Prevent Further Exposure

Keep other pets and animals away from the poisoned area to avoid additional incidents.

Step 9: Transport Safely

If the veterinarian advises you to bring your dog in, transport them safely. If the dog is unconscious, lay them on their right side during transportation.

Step 10: Do Not Delay

Time is critical in poisoning cases. Act quickly and seek professional veterinary help as soon as possible.

However, never attempt to retrieve an object if you can't see it or if it's lodged deep in the throat, as this may push it further down and worsen the situation.

Step 4: Perform Dog Heimlich Maneuver

If the object is not visible, you'll need to perform the dog version of the Heimlich maneuver. Follow these steps:

1- For Small Dogs:

- Place the dog on its back.
- Place one hand on the dog's back for support.
- With the other hand, apply quick and firm pressure just behind the dog's ribcage.
- Perform abdominal thrusts (similar to compressions) upward and inward to force the object out.
- Check the dog's mouth to see if the object is dislodged. Be prepared to perform several thrusts if necessary.

2- For Large Dogs:

- Stand behind the dog and wrap your arms around their abdomen.
- Make a fist with one hand and place it just above the dog's navel but below the ribcage.
- Place your other hand over the fist and perform quick, upward thrusts to dislodge the object.
- Check the dog's mouth to see if the object is dislodged. Repeat the thrusts if necessary.

Step 6: Seek Veterinary Help

Even if you manage to dislodge the object and the dog seems fine afterward, it is crucial to seek immediate veterinary attention. There might still be internal injuries or irritation caused by the object, and a professional evaluation is necessary to ensure the dog's well-being.

If you suspect your dog has food poisoning, follow these first aid steps:

Step 1: Remove the Source

: If you know what your dog ingested and it is still within reach, remove it immediately to prevent further consumption.

Step 2: DO NOT Induce Vomiting

Unlike with some types of poisoning, it is generally not recommended to induce vomiting in cases of food poisoning. Inducing vomiting can worsen the situation if your dog has ingested something caustic or sharp.

Step 3: Offer Fresh Water

Provide your dog with access to fresh, clean water to help keep them hydrated. However, do not force them to drink.

Step 4: Contact a Veterinarian

Food poisoning can be dangerous, and the severity of symptoms can escalate rapidly. Contact your veterinarian or an emergency animal clinic immediately for advice and instructions.

Step 5: Observe Your Dog

Keep a close eye on your dog's condition and note any changes in symptoms or behavior. This information will be helpful for the veterinarian in determining the appropriate course of action.

Step 6: Avoid Home Remedies

Do not attempt to treat food poisoning in dogs with home remedies or human medications, as this can worsen the situation.


Remember that the best course of action is always to seek professional veterinary care promptly. Even if your dog's symptoms seem mild at first, some toxins can have delayed effects and lead to serious complications if left untreated. Step 1: Move to a Cool Area

Immediately move your dog to a shady or air-conditioned location. Avoid direct sunlight and hot surfaces.

Step 2: Cool the Body

Begin cooling your dog's body by wetting them with cool (not cold) water. You can use a hose, wet towels, or immerse them in a shallow tub of cool water. Focus on the armpits, groin area, and foot pads as these areas have a higher concentration of blood vessels, which can help cool the body more effectively.

Step 3: Offer Water

Allow your dog to drink small amounts of cool water, but do not force them to drink.

Step 4: Use Fans or Air Conditioning

If available, use fans or air conditioning to aid in the cooling process.

Step 5: Avoid Ice-Cold Water or Ice Packs

DO NOT use ice-cold water or ice packs, as this may constrict blood vessels and hinder the cooling process.

Step 6: Monitor Rectal Temperature and Prevent Overcooling

If possible, monitor your dog's rectal temperature every few minutes. Do not overcool them, as hypothermia can be just as dangerous. Stop the cooling process once their temperature stabilizes around 39.4°C (103°F).

Step 7: Seek Veterinary Care

Even if your dog's temperature improves, it is crucial to seek immediate veterinary attention. Heatstroke can cause internal damage that may not be immediately apparent.

Step 8: Transport Safely

During transport to the veterinary clinic, continue to keep your dog in a cool environment and avoid letting them overheat again.


Remember, heatstroke is preventable with proper precautions. To avoid heatstroke in dogs:

- Never leave your dog in a parked car, even with the windows cracked open. The temperature inside a car can rise rapidly, leading to heatstroke.
- Avoid exercising your dog during the hottest parts of the day. Opt for early mornings or evenings when the weather is cooler.
- Always provide access to fresh, cool water, and ensure your dog has shade available during outdoor activities.
- Be mindful of hot pavement or surfaces, as they can burn your dog's paw pads.

Heatstroke is a medical emergency, and immediate first aid is crucial. Contact your veterinarian immediately for further guidance and treatment after providing first aid.

Shock is a lack of oxygen to the tissues of the body, usually caused by a fall in blood volume or blood pressure.

Shock occurs as a result of the body’s circulatory system failing to work properly, which means that the tissues and organs of the body, including the heart and the brain, struggle to get sufficient oxygen. The body’s response to this is to shut down the circulation to the skin, the heart speeds up as it tries to get sufficient blood supply and oxygen around the body and blood supply is drawn away from the gut to prioritise vital organs; which causes the animal to feel sick and thirsty and can lead to collapse.

Shock results from major drop in blood pressure and is serious.

If you suspect that your dog is in shock, it's essential to act quickly and seek immediate veterinary attention. Shock is a life-threatening condition that requires professional medical treatment. While waiting for professional help, follow these first aid steps to stabilize your dog:

Step 1: Keep Calm and Handle Gently

Approach your dog calmly and avoid sudden movements or loud noises. Handle your dog gently to avoid causing further stress or injury.

Step 2: Lay the Dog Down

Lay your dog down on their right side, as this can help improve blood flow to the heart.

Step 3: Elevate the Hindquarters

If your dog is having difficulty breathing or is unconscious, gently elevate their hindquarters to help with blood circulation. Do not force this if it causes discomfort.

Step 4: Keep Warm

Use a blanket or towel to keep your dog warm. Shock can cause a drop in body temperature, so maintaining warmth is essential.

Step 5: DO NOT Offer Food or Water

While your first instinct may be to offer food or water, do not give anything orally. Your dog may be unable to swallow properly or may aspirate the fluids, which could worsen the situation.

Step 6: Monitor Vital Signs

Check your dog's pulse, breathing rate, and gum color (capillary refill time). A normal gum color is pink, and the capillary refill time should be around 1-2 seconds.

Step 7: Keep the Dog Calm

Minimize stress and keep the environment as quiet and calm as possible. Excessive stimulation can worsen shock.

Step 8: Transport to the Veterinarian

Transport your dog to the nearest veterinary clinic or an emergency animal hospital immediately. Call ahead to let them know you are coming so they can be prepared for your arrival.
